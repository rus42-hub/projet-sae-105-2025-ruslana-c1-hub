When Merit Disappears Behind Gender: The Matilda Effect

Date de publication : 15 janvier 2025

Lecture : ~3–4 minutes

The Matilda Effect refers to the historical — and still ongoing — practice of attributing the achievements of female researchers to their male colleagues, or minimizing their contributions. This article explains where this bias comes from, how it operates, and what can be done to counter it.

Origins and Definition

The term was popularized by historian Margaret W. Rossiter (1993), referring to the work of Matilda Joslyn Gage (19th century). It describes the systematic omission, minimization, or appropriation of women’s roles in scientific research — often in favor of a male peer.

MMechanisms

Incorrect attribution

Discoveries credited to a better-known male scientist.

Minimization

Women’s work presented as “assistance.”.

Lower Visibility

Fewer citations, less access to prestigious journals.

Institutional Barriers & Stereotypes

Limited access to positions, funding, and networks.

Why It Persists

Unconscious bias, male-dominated professional circles, and reward systems (awards, promotions) all continue to sustain this injustice today. Recent studies have shown that academic papers signed by women are systematically under-cited when the author’s gender is identifiable.

What Can Be Done

Reexamine scientific history to restore forgotten contributions.

Encourage transparency (double-blind review, responsible citation practices).

Diversify committees and raise awareness from school age. These measures are neither magical nor immediate — but step by step, they narrow the gap.

« « The systematic denial of credit to women scientists is so pervasive that it deserves its own term: the Matilda Effect » » — M. W. Rossiter (1993).
