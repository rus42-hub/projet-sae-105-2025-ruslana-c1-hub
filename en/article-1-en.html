Sofia Kovalevskaïa:
Mathematics that Broke Barriers

Publication date: January 15, 2025

Reading time: ~3–4 minutes

The first woman to earn a doctorate in mathematics, Sofia Kovalevskaïa devoted her life to science despite the obstacles of her time.

1849 – An Extraordinary Childhood

Born in Moscow into an aristocratic family, Sofia Kovalevskaïa grew up in a world where women were expected to remain silent. Yet from a young age, she showed a deep fascination for science.

Her grandfather, an astronomer, passed on to her a love of mathematics, while her Polish family heritage instilled in her a profound sense of freedom. Barred from Russian universities, she studied on her own, reading lecture notes that her father had used to wallpaper her room — a quiet symbol of determined learning.

1868 – A Marriage for Knowledge

At nineteen, Sofia entered a fictional marriage with Vladimir Kovalevski, a young scientist. The arrangement allowed her to travel to Europe, where she first studied mathematics and physics at Heidelberg, despite official bans on female students.

In 1870, she moved to Berlin to study under Carl Weierstrass, one of the greatest mathematicians of his time. He quickly recognized her talent and became her mentor. In 1874, Sofia defended a groundbreaking thesis on differential equations and earned her doctorate from Göttingen — becoming the first woman in Europe to receive such a title.

1878–1884 – Between Struggle and Recognition

After years of rejection in Russia, Sofia accepts a position at the University of Stockholm — a first for a woman in a European institution.

1889 – Legacy of a Pioneer

Admired for both her intellect and her sensitivity, Sofia became known as “the princess of mathematics.” She also wrote short stories and plays, blending science with poetry.

« Each time I return to Russia, I feel as if I am leaving prison, » she once confessed...

She dreamed of serving the science of her homeland, but the doors remained closed to her. In 1889, she was finally recognized as a corresponding member of the Russian Academy of Sciences — an honor without a salary.

A few months later, Sofia died at 41 years old, leaving behind a scientific and literary legacy that continues to inspire today.
